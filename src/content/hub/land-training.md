## Why swimmers need land work

Swimming is wonderful for fitness but it has a blind spot: the water holds you up. Because it's non-weight-bearing, swimming alone doesn't fully develop the bone density, tendon strength and explosive power that racing demands, especially starts and turns, which depend on leg strength built on land.

Good dryland for age-group swimmers is swimming-specific: it complements the water work rather than copying a gym programme built for adults.

## The four pillars

**1. Core control.** Every stroke transfers power from arms to legs through the trunk. A soft core leaks speed. The staples: front and side planks, dead bugs, bird-dogs, streamline holds lying on the floor. Quality beats duration: a perfect 30-second plank is worth more than a sagging 2-minute one.

**2. Whole-body strength: bodyweight first.** Squats, lunges, press-ups, inverted rows, glute bridges. For age-group swimmers, mastering these with perfect control is the entire job. The old fear that strength work "stunts growth" is a myth. Properly supervised strength training is safe and good for children, and it's built into how Swim England's talent pathway trains young swimmers. The rule is simple: technical competency before load. Light resistance (bands, medicine balls, very light free weights) comes later, and heavier loading belongs in the senior years under qualified supervision.

**3. Plyometrics: the racing edge.** Jumping, bounding, skipping and hopping train the explosive leg power that fires you off the blocks and out of every turn. In a 100m race with two turns and a start, strong legs take time off. Always on forgiving surfaces, always with soft, quiet landings. The landing is the exercise.

**4. Mobility and flexibility.** Ankles that point, shoulders that rotate and a spine that extends are what make streamlines actually streamlined. Gentle, regular mobility work (never forced partner stretching) keeps range as swimmers grow.

## What sessions look like

A typical BASC land session runs around 30–45 minutes before or alongside water training. The warm-up follows the **RMAP** structure that Swim England and British Swimming publish for pre-pool work: Raise (heart rate), Mobilise (joints), Activate (key muscles), Prime (the movements you're about to swim):

- RMAP warm-up: skipping, arm swings, leg swings, band activation
- Circuit block: core, bodyweight strength and coordination stations
- Plyometric block (older squads): short, sharp, fully rested between efforts
- Band work: external rotation and scapular exercises that protect shoulders
- Cool-down and mobility

## What to wear and bring

- Clothes you can move freely in (shorts/leggings and a t-shirt)
- **Proper supportive trainers.** School plimsolls or fashion trainers don't cut it for jumping work
- A water bottle
- A resistance band if you've been given one

## The rules

1. **Technique before load, always.** A wobbly rep teaches your body the wrong pattern.
2. **Land training supports swimming. It doesn't replace it.** If time is short, the water comes first.
3. **Growth spurts change the rules.** Rapidly growing swimmers temporarily lose coordination; coaches scale the work accordingly. Say something if things feel off.
4. **No freelancing.** Don't add your own gym programme on top of squad land training without talking to your coach. More is not better.
