## Swimming and injury

Swimming is one of the safest sports a child can do. No tackles, no impact, no studs. But the volume is real: a swimmer in a hard training week takes thousands of strokes, and repetition is its own kind of load. The three areas that carry it are the shoulders, the knees (breaststrokers especially) and the lower back (butterfly and breaststroke).

Almost all swimming injuries are overuse injuries, which means most of them announce themselves quietly before they get loud. Catch them early.

## Swimmer's shoulder

The shoulder is the most mobile joint in the body, and swimming asks it to be mobile and powerful thousands of times a session. "Swimmer's shoulder" (irritation of the rotator cuff tendons) is the sport's classic complaint, and its most common injury.

**Prevention that works:**

- **Band work before swimming.** External rotations and scapular exercises wake up the small stabilising muscles before the big ones start pulling. Rotator cuff and scapular activation is built into the official Swim England/British Swimming pre-pool warm-up (the "Activate" stage of RMAP). This is why land warm-ups matter.
- **Technique is protection.** A crossed-over hand entry or a dropped elbow loads the tendon on every single stroke. When a coach corrects your catch, they're also protecting your shoulder.
- **Balanced strength.** The rowing, posture and scapular exercises in land training exist because swimmers develop strong fronts and comparatively weak backs. Swim England's land training programmes include dedicated scapular stability sessions.
- **Sensible paddle use.** Paddles multiply load. Only use the size your coach sets, and never with a sore shoulder.

**The warning sign:** an ache that appears during swimming and starts changing your stroke, shortening your reach, avoiding one arm. That's the moment to tell the coach, not three weeks later.

## Breaststroker's knee

The whip kick loads the inside of the knee in a way no other stroke does. Knee pain is the sport's second most common complaint, with breaststrokers consistently the most affected. Protection is straightforward: vary your strokes in training (the programme does this deliberately; heavy early specialisation in breaststroke is what raises the risk most), build the hip and core strength land training provides, and flag any inside-knee pain early. Breaststrokers with sore knees can usually keep training fully on other strokes while it settles.

## Growing bodies

Growth plates are the working ends of growing bones and they're temporarily more vulnerable than the tendons attached to them. Two things follow:

1. **Pain near joints in a growing swimmer always deserves attention.** Heel pain (Sever's) and knee pain (Osgood-Schlatter) are common, manageable, and much better handled early.
2. **During a growth spurt, expect a temporary dip.** Limbs grow before coordination catches up. Times can stall for a term. It's biology, not lost talent, and pushing harder through it is the wrong response.

## The rules we ask every swimmer to follow

1. **Never hide pain to avoid missing a gala.** A swimmer who reports a niggle on Monday usually races Saturday. A swimmer who hides it until it's a real injury misses the season.
2. **Do the boring stuff.** Band work, warm-ups and mobility are the unglamorous 10% that protects the other 90%.
3. **Pain that changes your technique means stop and tell someone.** Discomfort from effort is training. Pain that alters your stroke is damage you need to stop before it gets worse.
4. **See a professional for anything persistent.** Coaches can adjust training but they aren't physios. A GP or chartered physiotherapist should look at anything lasting more than a couple of weeks.
