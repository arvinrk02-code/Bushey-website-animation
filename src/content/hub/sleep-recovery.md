## Why sleep is a training session

You don't get fitter during training. Training is the stimulus; the body adapts afterwards, mostly while you sleep. Deep sleep is when growth hormone is released and when the brain consolidates what you practised. That turn you drilled on Wednesday gets wired in on Wednesday night. Swim England's sports science team puts sleep first for recovery, and with good reason.

Cut the sleep and it shows: flat training, more colds, more niggles. Research on teenage athletes has found that those regularly sleeping under 8 hours a night get injured noticeably more often than those sleeping 8 or more.

## How much is enough?

The standard sleep guidance for young people:

- **Ages 6–12:** 9–12 hours per night
- **Teenagers (13–18):** 8–10 hours per night

Most young athletes miss these numbers. Training load and late sessions are the usual culprits, which is why swimmers have to be deliberate about it.

Do the maths. If a swimmer trains until 9pm, gets home at 9:40pm, and needs to be up at 7am, the numbers only work with a disciplined wind-down. That's a family logistics problem as much as a swimmer one, and worth sorting out.

## The post-training wind-down

Evening training is the reality of club swimming, and adrenaline plus bright pool lighting can make it hard to switch off. What works:

- **Eat your recovery snack on the way home**, not at 10pm in the kitchen.
- **Shower warm.** The body-temperature drop afterwards is a natural sleep trigger.
- **Screens away after you get home.** Blue light plus a group chat is the opposite of a wind-down. Charge the phone outside the bedroom if you can.
- **Same routine, same order, every time.** Bodies respond well to predictability; the routine itself becomes the sleep signal.
- **Keep the room cool and dark.**

## Recovery beyond sleep

- **Rest days matter.** The programme includes them because adaptation needs space. A rest day spent at a trampoline party isn't a rest day.
- **Easy means easy.** When the coach sets recovery swimming, swimming it hard doesn't make you tough. It makes Thursday's main set worse.
- **Eat and drink to recover.** See the [Nutrition](/athlete-hub/nutrition) page. Recovery is mostly sleep plus food, in that order.
- **Stretching and mobility.** A few minutes of gentle mobility after sessions helps, especially through the shoulders and ankles. See [Injury prevention](/athlete-hub/injury-prevention).
- **Growth spurts change everything.** Rapidly growing swimmers are temporarily clumsier, more injury-prone and more tired. It's normal. Tell the coach. Training can flex around it.

## Spotting under-recovery

Watch for this pattern: times plateauing or going backwards, permanent tiredness, irritability, niggles that won't clear, and dreading sessions that used to be fun. That usually means under-recovery, not laziness. The fix is more sleep and food, not more effort. If it persists, talk to your coach. Adjusting a week of training early beats losing a month later.
